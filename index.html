Fig. 03 — Motivation & Benefit

Traditional rockets rely on fuel combustion to reach the upper atmosphere — expensive and environmentally costly. SpinLaunch's centrifuge-based approach stores energy mechanically instead, spinning a payload up to release speed before letting it go, removing the need for a chemical burn during that phase of launch. This model demonstrates that mechanism at a small, interactive scale, aimed at UIUC's Engineering Open House.

Fig. 04 — Mechanism Breakdown

01 — Rotation

A DC motor drives a rotating arm with the projectile mounted at its end. As the arm spins up, the projectile builds centrifugal acceleration; the faster it spins, the more kinetic energy it stores before release. Reaching a specific target trajectory means hitting a precalculated RPM, tuned through repeated test launches. The arm itself has to be rigid enough to survive the impact load at the moment of release, and it has to carry both the motor and the holding/release mechanism at its tip without flexing.

02 — Holding-Release

A claw fixed to a rod grips the projectile through the spin-up phase. The rod is mounted at the center of the rotating body. Once the arm hits its target rotational speed, a trigger mounted beside the firing tube drops and collides with the rod — the collision separates the rod from the claw, releasing the projectile. From that point, there's no more centripetal force acting on it, so by Newton's first law it flies off in a straight tangent line before gravity takes over and turns it into projectile motion.

Fig. 05 — Landing Calculation

Once released, the projectile's velocity and launch angle determine where it lands — standard projectile-motion physics. We used that relationship to predict landing range, then built in a standard deviation to account for real-world uncertainty: wind, the projectile's own characteristics, ambient temperature, and (for the parachute-equipped version) parachute deployment timing.
