Motivation & benefit

Traditional rockets rely on fuel combustion to reach the upper atmosphere — expensive and environmentally costly. SpinLaunch's centrifuge-based approach stores energy mechanically instead, spinning a payload up to release speed before letting it go, removing the need for a chemical burn during that phase of launch. This model demonstrates that mechanism at a small, interactive scale, built for EOH.

Rotation

A DC motor drives a rotating arm with the projectile mounted at its end. As the arm spins up, the projectile builds centrifugal acceleration — reaching a specific target trajectory means hitting a precalculated RPM, tuned through repeated test launches. The arm has to be rigid enough to survive the impact load at release, and carry the holding/release mechanism at its tip without flexing.

Holding-Release

A claw fixed to a rod grips the projectile through spin-up. Once the arm hits its target speed, a trigger beside the firing tube drops and collides with the rod — separating it from the claw and releasing the projectile. From that point there's no more centripetal force acting on it, so by Newton's first law it flies off in a straight tangent line before gravity turns it into projectile motion.

Landing calculation

Once released, the projectile's velocity and launch angle determine where it lands — standard projectile-motion physics. We used that relationship to predict landing range, then built in a standard deviation to account for real-world uncertainty: wind, the projectile's own characteristics, ambient temperature, and parachute deployment timing.
